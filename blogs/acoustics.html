Fundamentals of Acoustic Wave Equations

A foundational understanding of how sound propagates in a medium

Introduction

Acoustic wave propagation forms the basis of numerous engineering applications, including medical ultrasound, sonar systems, and non-destructive testing. Understanding the governing equations allows us to model how pressure waves travel through different media.

Key Physical Quantities

  • Pressure (p): Deviation from ambient pressure
  • Particle Velocity (u): Velocity of fluid particles
  • Density (ρ): Mass per unit volume
  • Speed of Sound (c): Propagation speed of waves in medium

Governing Equations

1. Continuity Equation

Represents conservation of mass in a compressible medium:

∂ρ/∂t + ∇·(ρu) = 0

2. Momentum Equation (Euler’s Equation)

Derived from Newton’s second law:

ρ (∂u/∂t + u·∇u) = -∇p

3. Equation of State

Relates pressure and density variations:

p = c²ρ

Linear Acoustic Approximation

For small perturbations, nonlinear terms can be neglected. This leads to simplified linear equations where acoustic pressure and velocity are treated as small deviations from equilibrium.

Derivation of the Wave Equation

Combining the continuity equation and momentum equation under linear assumptions leads to the classical acoustic wave equation:

∂²p/∂t² = c² ∇²p

This equation describes how pressure waves propagate through space and time.

Physical Interpretation

  • Wave speed depends on medium properties
  • Solutions represent traveling or standing waves
  • Boundary conditions determine real-world behavior

Applications

  • Medical Ultrasound Imaging
  • HIFU (High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound)
  • Sonar Systems
  • Non-destructive Testing

Beyond the Linear Model

In high-intensity regimes such as HIFU, nonlinear effects become significant. More advanced models like the Westervelt equation and KZK equation are used to describe attenuation, diffraction, and nonlinear propagation.

Conclusion

The acoustic wave equation provides the foundation for understanding sound propagation. Mastery of these fundamentals is essential for advanced topics such as ultrasound simulation, transducer design, and acoustic field characterization.
